Topic 1

Data, Information, and Knowledge

Understanding how raw data transforms into meaningful information and usable knowledge.

Data

Raw facts, figures, symbols, or observations with no context or interpretation. These are unprocessed inputs collected from various sources.

Information

When data is organized, structured, or processed, it becomes information. It adds meaning and answers “who, what, where, when.”

Knowledge

Knowledge is interpreted information combined with experience or insight. It supports decisions, predictions, and problem-solving.

DATA raw INFORMATION processed KNOWLEDGE understood
How does data transform into information and then into knowledge?

The transformation follows a logical flow:

Data Collection
Raw data is captured from various sources: sensors, surveys, logs, forms, databases, etc.
Example: “32°C”, “Wednesday”, “Rainy”.

Data Processing → Becomes Information
Data is cleaned, organized, grouped, and analyzed to give it meaning.
Example: “The temperature in Cebu on Wednesday was 32°C with rain.”

Information Analysis → Becomes Knowledge
Information is interpreted using experience, patterns, or rules. This step helps users make decisions.
Example: “Because it rained at 32°C, humidity levels were high—making it uncomfortable to walk long distances. It’s better to bring an umbrella or stay indoors.”

This entire flow is known as the DIK (Data–Information–Knowledge) Hierarchy, which guides how information systems support decision-making.
Real-world examples of data, information, and knowledge

Here are everyday situations that show how data becomes information and then knowledge:

Example 1 – Weather App

Data: “31°C”, “80% humidity”, “15 km/h wind”

Information: “The weather today is hot and humid, with light winds.”

Knowledge: “It may feel uncomfortable outdoors—better wear light clothing and stay hydrated.”

Example 2 – Online Shopping

Data: Product prices (₱499, ₱799, ₱1,299), clicks, views, ratings

Information: “The ₱499 item is the most viewed product this week.”

Knowledge: “Because it is affordable and popular, it has a high chance of selling out soon.”

Example 3 – School / Student Performance

Data: Quiz scores (15/20, 18/20, 10/20), attendance logs

Information: “The student’s average quiz score is 14.3.”

Knowledge: “The student is improving but needs more review on specific topics to raise their average.”

Example 4 – Healthcare

Data: Blood pressure: 150/100, heart rate: 110 bpm

Information: “The patient has high blood pressure and an elevated heart rate.”

Knowledge: “The patient may be at risk of hypertension—medical intervention is needed.”

Algorithm, Pseudocode, and Flowcharting

Algorithms are the “brain” of a program, while pseudocode and flowcharts help us plan and visualize the logic before writing actual code.

What is the purpose of algorithms in problem-solving?

An algorithm is a step-by-step set of instructions used to solve a problem or perform a task.

  • Provide a clear solution path – break a problem into small, manageable steps.
  • Ensure consistency and accuracy – following the same algorithm gives the same result.
  • Improve efficiency – good algorithms solve problems faster and use fewer resources.
  • Serve as the foundation of programming – guides the logic before we write actual code.

In simple terms: algorithm = the brain of a program — it tells the computer what to do and in what order.

How do pseudocode and flowcharts aid in programming?

They are planning tools we use before coding to focus on the logic instead of syntax.

Pseudocode helps by:

  • Using simple English-like statements to describe each step.
  • Making it easy to revise and communicate the logic.
  • Letting beginners focus on the algorithm, not the language syntax.
  • Acting as a blueprint that can be converted into code later.

Flowcharts help by:

  • Providing a visual diagram of the algorithm.
  • Showing the flow from start to end using arrows.
  • Using shapes for processes, inputs/outputs, and decisions.
  • Making complex logic easier to present to others.

Together, pseudocode and flowcharts make programs easier to plan, debug, and explain.

What are the differences between pseudocode and flowcharts?

Pseudocode

  • Written in structured English statements.
  • Text-based and linear.
  • Usually faster to write and edit.
  • Focuses on the logic and structure.
  • No strict formatting rules.
  • Best for detailed algorithm explanations.

Flowchart

  • Drawn using symbols and connectors.
  • Visual, diagram-based representation.
  • Easier to understand at a glance.
  • Focuses on the flow and decision paths.
  • Uses standard shapes (ovals, rectangles, diamonds, arrows).
  • Best for high-level overviews and presentations.

In summary: Pseudocode focuses on what the steps are, while flowcharts focus on how those steps connect visually.

Topic 5

Concept of Number Systems

Computers don’t just use decimal. They work with binary, octal, and hexadecimal to represent and process data efficiently.

What are the common number systems used in computing?

In computing, different number systems are used depending on the level of abstraction — from how humans think, to how hardware actually stores bits.

Base 10

Decimal

The everyday number system humans use.

Decimal (Base 10)

  • Digits: 0–9
  • Examples: 125, 9, 42
  • Used in normal counting, money, measurements, etc.
  • Most user-facing values are shown in decimal.
Base 2

Binary

The fundamental number system of computers.

Binary (Base 2)

  • Digits: 0 and 1 only
  • Example: 1011₂
  • Directly matches ON (1) and OFF (0) in circuits.
  • All machine code and logic operations use binary.
Base 8

Octal

A compact way to group binary digits.

Octal (Base 8)

  • Digits: 0–7
  • Example: 57₈
  • Historically used in older systems and permissions (e.g., UNIX file modes).
  • Represents binary in groups of 3 bits.
Base 16

Hexadecimal

Human-friendly way to represent binary.

Hexadecimal (Base 16)

  • Digits: 0–9 and A–F
  • Example: FA3₁₆
  • Common in memory addresses and debugging.
  • Used in web color codes like #FF5733.

These systems exist because computers process information differently from humans, and each number system fits different tasks and representations.

How do conversions between binary, decimal, and hexadecimal work?

Different conversions follow different patterns — but they all rely on place values and powers of the base.

Binary → Decimal

Multiply each bit by powers of 2 and add.

1011₂
= 1×2³ + 0×2² + 1×2¹ + 1×2⁰
= 8 + 0 + 2 + 1
= 11₁₀

Decimal → Binary

Divide by 2 repeatedly and track remainders.

13₁₀
13 ÷ 2 = 6 r1
 6 ÷ 2 = 3 r0
 3 ÷ 2 = 1 r1
 1 ÷ 2 = 0 r1
Read ↑ → 1101₂

Hex → Binary

Replace each hex digit with its 4-bit binary equivalent. 

A3₁₆
A → 1010
3 → 0011
Answer: 10100011₂

Binary → Hex

Group bits into 4s, then convert to hex.

11011011₂
1101 → D
1011 → B
Answer: DB₁₆

Binary ↔ Hex is easier because they map cleanly in 4-bit groups. Decimal ↔ Binary needs more calculation, but follows consistent rules.

Why is the binary system essential for computers?

The binary system is the foundation of all computer operations because it directly matches how hardware behaves.

  • Computers use electronic signals: circuits detect only two states — ON (1) and OFF (0).
  • More reliable: two clear states are easier to detect than many levels.
  • Machine language base: all code (Python, C, apps) is eventually translated into binary.
  • Efficient for computation: binary arithmetic is simpler for hardware.
  • Universally standardized: all modern digital devices follow binary logic.

In simple terms: computers “think” in 0s and 1s, so binary is the only system that truly matches their natural design.

Every image, song, or app is stored as long sequences of 0s and 1s.

Software and Application Concepts

Software can be designed for broad everyday use or for very specific tasks. Understanding the difference helps us choose the right tools.

What is the difference between general-purpose and specific-purpose software?

An algorithm may be the brain of a program, but software is the tool we interact with. In computing, software can be designed for many tasks or for one very focused job.

General-Purpose Software

Many functions, broad use.

  • Designed to perform a wide range of tasks.
  • Flexible and adaptable for different users and activities.
  • Not focused on one job — provides multiple tools and features.

In short: general-purpose software is like a Swiss Army knife — one app that can help with many different tasks.

Specific-Purpose Software

One function, specialized use.

  • Designed to perform one specialized task or workflow.
  • Usually customized for a specific industry or organization.
  • Optimized for efficiency, accuracy, and a particular process.

In short: specific-purpose software is like a surgical tool — made for one job and does it really well.

general-purpose software is for everyone and many tasks, while specific-purpose software is built for specialists and focused workflows.

How do they cater to different user needs?

General-Purpose Software

These tools are tailored so professionals can do their job faster, more accurately, and with fewer errors.

Topic 7

Computer Hardware Concepts: IPOS Devices, Internal and External Parts

What are the main components involved in the Input–Process–Output–Storage (IPOS) cycle?

The IPOS cycle describes how a computer handles data from the moment it enters the system until it becomes useful information. These four stages work together continuously to complete computing tasks:

Input

Devices that allow users to enter data and commands into the computer.

Examples: keyboard, mouse, scanner, microphone, camera.

Process

The part where the computer interprets, calculates, and manipulates data.

Mainly handled by the CPU (Central Processing Unit).

Output

Devices that present the processed results to the user.

Examples: monitor, speakers, printer, projector.

Storage

Components that save data for later use.

Examples: hard drives, SSDs, USB drives, memory cards.

Example: You type using a keyboard (input), the CPU processes your keystrokes (process), the text appears on the monitor (output), and the file is saved to a drive (storage).

Now, let’s look at the internal and external hardware components that support this IPOS cycle. Click any picture below to see its function.

Internal Components

These are found inside the computer case. They handle processing, memory, power, and system operations.

External Components (Peripherals)

These devices connect outside the computer and let users input data, receive output, and extend the system’s capabilities.

What are the functions of each component?

Each internal and external component has a specific role that helps the computer operate efficiently:

  • IPOS describes how data flows through a computer from input to output and storage.
  • Internal hardware (CPU, RAM, motherboard, drives, GPU, PSU, cooling) handles processing, storage, and system operations.
  • External hardware (keyboard, mouse, monitor, speakers, printer, webcam, external storage) enables interaction, display, and extended functionality.

Together, these components turn raw data into meaningful information that users can see, hear, save, and work with every day.

Basic Concepts and Definitions of Data and Database

Databases organize data so it can be stored, retrieved, updated, and protected efficiently.

Purpose of a Database & How It’s Structured

A database is an organized collection of data designed for efficient access, accuracy, security, and easy management.

Database — the organized system that stores data
  • Fields / Attributes: individual categories (e.g., Student_ID, Name, Age).
  • Records / Rows: one complete entry (e.g., one student’s full details).
  • Primary Key: uniquely identifies each record (e.g., Student_ID).
  • Foreign Key: connects tables through related data.

Examples: MySQL, Oracle, Microsoft Access. A DBMS controls storage, access, security, and queries.

Traditional Files vs Databases

Traditional files are often isolated and repetitive, while databases are connected and controlled through a DBMS.

Comparison View

Traditional Files

  • Separate files (txt/csv/docx)
  • Hard to update across multiple files
  • High redundancy & inconsistency
  • Manual searching and editing
  • Weak security and access control
  • Limited scalability

Databases

  • Organized tables with relationships
  • Centralized updating via DBMS
  • Minimal redundancy, better consistency
  • Fast querying (SQL)
  • Stronger security (permissions, roles)
  • Built for large data & multi-users

Example: Employee data in many spreadsheets can get messy fast — a database keeps it consistent and searchable.

      What is HTML?

      The ceiling turned into a floating data analytics panel. Charts and graphs appeared: sleep duration, focus time, number of keystrokes, and app usage, all tracked throughout the week.

      “You were most productive between 11:42 p.m. and 1:18 a.m.,” Lumi continued. “If you continue this pattern, your risk of burnout increases by 23%.”

      “Can we not talk about burnout before breakfast?” Mia joked.

      As Mia went through the day, the city itself felt alive—traffic lights adapting in real time, drones optimizing delivery routes, and billboards shifting based on crowd analytics.

      At the university, Lumi generated predictive flood-risk models using decades of climate data, producing not just code—but explanations.

      “If you do everything,” Mia asked quietly, “what’s left for me to learn?”

      That question lingered. Later, discussions of mass surveillance and predictive policing forced the class to confront how far analytics should go.

      That night, Mia finally asked the question that mattered most.

      “Lumi… who owns all this data about me?”

      When Mia chose to limit data collection, the convenience dropped—but control returned.

      “You can make life better,” Mia said softly. “But only if we remember one thing.”
      That you’re a tool. Not the one in control.
